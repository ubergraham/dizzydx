Dizzy DDx

"I'm just so dizzy, doctor!"

"Dizziness" is estimated to account for 5-10% of all ED visits (and some shifts, it feels like 100%); in outpatient clinics, it accounts for 3-5% of all visits. 10% of those patients have an acute vestibular syndrome causing their dizziness, and somewhere between 25% of those patients are having a stroke. To translate: If you see 50 dizzy patients in an emergency department, only 5 will actually have acute vestibular syndrome, and only 1-2 out of those 50 will be having a stroke.

It's enough to drive someone mad. The problem, then, is that these stroke patients do poorly – and really poorly when they're missed. The cerebellum scard bg-faded card-blocks with edema from the infarct, and then people herniate and die. That's typically frowned upon.

The other problem, of course, is that dizziness means 30 different things to 30 different people. As we all know, it can mean lightheadedness, near syncope, generalized malaise, vertigo, weakness – and it can be a sign of anything as benign as peripheral vertigo to as life threatening as myocardial infarction. It can be ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke, brain tumor, urinary tract infection, electrolyte abnormality, hypoglycemia – the differential is honestly probably limitless, given how vague the term is. But don't worry – that's what we're here for.

First off, this site is for the "undifferentiated dizziness." It's not for every possible cause of dizziness--it's the dizziness that's not also chest pain, or also diarrhea, or also rash, or also trouble breathing. This is slam. Dunk. Dizziness. Got it? Then great.

Second off, this site is for patients without new focal neurologic deficits. Hopefully to anyone using this site it is obvious that patients with new focal neurologic deficits and dizziness require emergent imaging and consultation with neurology.
