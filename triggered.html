Triggered vestibular syndromes make up the vast majority of neurologically dizzy patients.

The most common of these diagnoses is benign paroxysmal positional vertigo. About 2.4% of individuals will have BPPV sometime during their lifetime (3.2% in females, 1.6% in males). 1 It is commonly treated pharmaceutically with antihistamine/anticholinergic medicines like meclizine, or benzodiazepenes like lorazepam. It is mechanically treated with canalith repositioning exercises like the Epley manuever or the half-somersault manuever (a modified Epley).

A less common cause of triggered dizziness is orthostatic hypotension, from hypovolemia (dehydration, hemorrhage), autonomic dysfunction, or medications (most classically, alpha-1 blockers). These patients typically become dizzy when going from lying sitting standing and improve when they return to a lying position.

Finally, a rare cause of triggered vertigo is tumor, particularly posterior fossa tumors. These patients often may present with headache and vomiting and typically have had symptoms for weeks-months, as one would expect with a slow-growing intracranial mass.
