Dizzy DDx

First, we're trying to decide what kind of vestibular syndrome the patient has.
Please choose from one of the four options:

Triggered

Triggered vestibular syndromes are when a patient has something that triggers their dizziness symptoms. They might have no symptoms at rest but when they move their head, this triggers their dizziness symptoms to return. If they stop moving their head, their symptoms resolve completely, but can be re-triggered by the trigger. Note: This is not the same thing as a certain trigger exacerbating the dizziness.

DDx Includes:

  • BPPV
  • Tumor
  • Orthostatic Hypotension

Management Often Includes:

  • Dix-Hallpike and/or Epley Manuevers
  • Meclizine or other Anti-Vertigo Medicine
  • Possible neuroimaging for tumor
Patient Has Triggered Dizziness

Episodic

Episodic vestibular syndromes are when a patient has dizziness intermittnetly. They may have very severe symptoms but they come and go -- if their "episode" is resolved when you see them, they should have no more dizziness.

DDx Includes:

  • Vestibular Migraine
  • Arrythmia
  • Posterior Circulation TIA

Management Often Includes:

  • Cardiac Evaluation
  • Possible neuroimaging for TIA risk
Patient Has Episodic Dizziness

Acute and Constant

Acute and Constant vestibular sydnromes don't come and go. They're present and not going away. They may have a component of position — in that position change may exacerbate a patient's symptoms — but this is different than position triggering symptoms that are completely absent until the trigger.

DDx Includes:

  • Vestibular Neuritis
  • Labrynthitis
  • Posterior Circulation Stroke

Management Often Includes:

  • MRI neuroimaging for posterior circulation stroke
  • Steroids for vestibular neuritis/labrynthitis
Patient Has Acute and Constant Dizziness

Mixed or Unclear Dizziness

Some patients will simply not fit into a particular category which can significantly challenge the clinician. Most experts recommend using the neurologic exam, patient age and risk factors for stroke, and overall clinical picture to determine if the patient should receive imaging.
